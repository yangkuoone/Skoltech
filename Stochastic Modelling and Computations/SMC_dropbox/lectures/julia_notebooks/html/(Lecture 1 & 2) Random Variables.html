Lecture I: Random Variables: Characterization & Description¶

Discreate events¶

Bernoulli distribution is a probability distribution of a random variable which takes the value 11 with success probability of pp and the value 00 with failure probability of q=1−pq=1−p. It can be used to represent a coin toss where 11 and 00 would represent "head" and "tail" (or vice versa), respectively.

Binomial distribution with parameters nn and pp is the discrete probability distribution of the number of successes in a sequence of nn independent yes/no experiments, each of which yields success with probability pp. When n=1n=1, the binomial distribution is a Bernoulli distribution.

Let us see how the histogram of the Binominal distribution with n=100,p=0.25n=100,p=0.25 looks like - by tossing the coin N=10000N=10000 times.

In general, if the random variable XX follows the binomial distribution with parameters n∈Nn∈N and p∈[0,1]p∈[0,1], we write X∼B(n,p)X∼B(n,p). The probability of getting exactly kk successes in nn trials is given by the probability mass function:

f(k|n,p)=Pr(X=k)=(nk)pk(1−p)n−kfor k=0,1,2,...,n,f(k|n,p)=Pr(X=k)=(nk)pk(1−p)n−kfor k=0,1,2,...,n,
where (nk)=n!k!(n−k)!(nk)=n!k!(n−k)! is the binomial coefficient, hence the name of the distribution.

Continuous distributions¶

Gaussian distribution is the most famous, and most important, distribution of a continuous variable. The probability density of the normal distribution is:

f(x|μ,σ)=1σ2π−−√e−(x−μ)22σ2.f(x|μ,σ)=1σ2πe−(x−μ)22σ2.
Here, μμ is the mean and σσ is the variance. A random variable sampled from a Gaussian distribution can also be refeerred to as normally distributed.

Just for fun you can also play a "reconstruction/learning" game. Create samples from a distribution and try to reconstruct parameters of the distribution from the samples (think of two rooms, one where samples were created and another one where they were received and tasked to reconstruct the distribution).

using Distributions

x = linspace(-10, 10, 200)
d=Cauchy()
y=pdf(d,x)
PyPlot.plot(x,y,"ro")

samples=rand(d,100)             # play with the number of samples - to see improvement
d1=fit(Cauchy,samples)
y1=pdf(d1,x)

Central limit theorem¶

The central limit theorem (CLT) states that, given certain conditions, the arithmetic mean of a sufficiently large number of independent random variables, each with a well-defined expected value and well-defined variance, will be approaching asymptotically a normal/Gaussian distribution.

Let us consider NN samples, xixi, of the exponentially distributed random variable, x∼exp(−x)x∼exp⁡(−x).

using PyPlot
using Distributions

N = 10000 # number of terms in X_N 
length = 10000 # the volume of histogram data - number of trials
xn = Array(Float64, length) # realization of random variable X_N

#d = Exponential(1) # one example of a distribution
d=LogNormal(0.5)  # another example

m=mean(d)
v=sqrt(var(d))


for i in 1:length

    xn[i] = sqrt(N)*(sum(x_values)/N-m) # \sqrt{N}(\sum_i^N x_i/N-\mu)
